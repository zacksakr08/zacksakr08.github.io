The Landmark

The place. The history. The meaning.

Financial District (FIDI)

📍 [FiDi , New York City]

This is one of the most recognizable and meaningful places in all of New York City. Whether you've visited once or a thousand times, there is something about this place that stays with you long after you've left.

Its history stretches back generations, and its walls hold stories of countless New Yorkers — the hopeful, the hurried, the heartbroken, and the triumphant.

FiDi (Financial District) in Lower Manhattan is one of the oldest parts of New York City, originally settled by the Dutch in the 1600s as New Amsterdam, with Wall Street literally named after a wooden wall the Dutch built for protection. It became the financial heart of the United States in the late 18th century when the New York Stock Exchange was founded there in 1792, drawing banks and trading firms that cemented its global importance. Today it's one of the most densely packed business districts in the world, though it has increasingly transformed into a mixed-use neighborhood with residents, restaurants, and tourists alongside its iconic financial institutions.

FiDi's architecture is a dramatic mix of eras. You have early 20th-century skyscrapers like the Woolworth Building (1913) in ornate Gothic Revival style sitting right next to modern glass towers. Notable design features include elaborate terra cotta facades, neoclassical columns on old bank buildings, and Art Deco details on buildings like 40 Wall Street. Much of the area underwent significant restoration after 9/11, which destroyed or damaged several structures, and more recently many old office buildings have been converted and restored into residential lofts and apartments, preserving their historic exteriors while modernizing the interiors.

To New Yorkers, FiDi represents the city's roots and resilience, a place where the city was born and where the community came together after 9/11. To the world, it symbolizes global capitalism through the New York Stock Exchange and Wall Street, while Ground Zero gives it a deeper human significance that resonates far beyond the city.
